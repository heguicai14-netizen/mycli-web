Operational Transformation (OT): A Beginner's Primer

Operational Transformation, almost universally abbreviated OT, is a family of algorithms for keeping multiple replicas of a shared document in sync as users edit them concurrently. OT predates CRDTs by about two decades and was the technology behind the first widely-used collaborative editor, Jupiter (Xerox PARC), as well as the well-known Google Wave and the original Google Docs.

The core idea

The premise of OT is simple: every user edit is expressed as an operation (for example, insert "x" at position 5), and when an operation arrives at a replica that has already applied other operations, the incoming op is transformed against each intervening op so that it produces the right effect on the new state. If Alice inserts at position 5 and Bob, who has not seen Alice's edit yet, inserts at position 3, then Bob's op, when it reaches Alice, applies cleanly at position 3; Alice's op, when it reaches Bob, must be transformed to apply at position 6 (shifted right by Bob's insertion).

Why a transformation function

The transformation function, traditionally written T(op_a, op_b), must satisfy two properties. TP1 requires that applying op_a then the transformed op_b yields the same state as applying op_b then the transformed op_a; this ensures the two replicas converge. TP2 is a more subtle condition needed when three or more concurrent ops are present; satisfying TP2 turns out to be remarkably hard, and many published OT algorithms have been shown to violate it.

OT vs CRDTs

Both OT and CRDTs solve roughly the same problem (concurrent edits that converge), but they make different design trade-offs. OT typically relies on a central server to assign a total order to operations, which keeps the per-op data structures small and simple but makes truly peer-to-peer or offline-first deployments awkward. CRDTs encode the merge logic into the data itself and need no central authority, at the cost of additional metadata per element.

Where OT is still used

Despite the academic ascendancy of CRDTs, OT remains the technology of choice for many production collaborative editors with a star topology: Google Docs, ShareDB, and ot.js all use OT. The combination of a single source of truth (the server) and a comparatively small per-document footprint makes OT a pragmatic fit for SaaS document tools.

Recommended starting reading

"Operational Transformation in Real-Time Group Editors" by Sun and Ellis (1998) is the canonical introduction. For an implementer's view, the ShareDB documentation walks through a working OT system in a readable amount of detail.
