Sequence CRDTs and Their Engineering Trade-offs

Counters and sets are the textbook examples, but the CRDTs that show up in production systems most often are sequence CRDTs — the data structures behind collaborative rich-text editors. Designing one that is correct, fast, and memory-bounded is much harder than the primer makes it look.

The fundamental challenge

A sequence is an ordered list of elements (typically characters in a document). When two replicas concurrently insert at the "same" position — say, both insert a character between positions 5 and 6 — the merge must produce a deterministic order without either replica having observed the other's edit. The trick is to assign each inserted element an identifier that totally orders all possible positions, even ones invented concurrently.

Major families

  • WOOT (Without Operational Transformation) — the first widely-cited sequence CRDT. Each character carries unique IDs of its predecessor and successor at insertion time. Correct but memory-heavy and slow for large documents.
  • Logoot / LSEQ — assign each position a path of integers in a virtual tree. Inserts allocate a new path between the neighbours. Compact in the average case, but adversarial edit patterns can cause path lengths to grow without bound.
  • Treedoc — positions are nodes in a binary tree. Concurrent inserts become siblings in a deterministic order.
  • RGA (Replicated Growable Array) — each element has a timestamp and a reference to the element it was inserted after; tombstones mark deletions. Used as the basis of Automerge's text type.
  • Yjs — a modern, highly-optimised RGA-like CRDT with run-length encoding of adjacent inserts by the same client and aggressive garbage collection of tombstones.

Tombstones and garbage collection

Every sequence CRDT must remember deletions: a removed element cannot simply be dropped, because a concurrent replica might be inserting next to it. The remembered placeholder is called a tombstone. Naively, tombstones accumulate forever and the document grows unboundedly. Practical implementations garbage-collect tombstones once they can prove no concurrent reference can still arrive — usually by reaching causal stability across all known replicas, which requires either a synchronous barrier or a vector-clock check.

Performance engineering

For interactive editing, the CRDT must process a local keystroke in well under one frame (~16ms) on documents of millions of characters. Achieving this typically requires: run-length compression of contiguous edits, a separate spatial index for cursor positioning, lazy materialisation of the visible string, and a careful split between the slow merge path and the fast local-edit path. Yjs and Automerge spend most of their engineering effort here, not on the algorithmic core.

When not to use a CRDT

If your application's writes are inherently centralised — every write goes through a single authoritative server — an Operational Transformation system, or even simple optimistic locking with retries, may be simpler and faster. CRDTs pay an ongoing complexity tax that is only worth it when peer-to-peer or offline-first semantics are genuinely required.
