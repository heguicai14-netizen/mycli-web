Designing Resilient Background Jobs

A short field report on running long-lived workers in production environments.

Why background jobs are hard

Background jobs sit at the boundary between request/response code and the messy real world. They survive deploys, they survive partial outages, and — if you are not careful — they survive your attempts to kill them. A well-designed worker treats every step as recoverable and every external call as potentially flaky.

Idempotency keys

The single most useful primitive is the idempotency key: a deterministic identifier attached to the unit of work. If a step retries, the downstream system can recognise the second attempt as a duplicate and short-circuit. We typically derive the key from the job id plus the step name.

Backoff and jitter

Exponential backoff with full jitter remains the boring-but-correct default. It avoids the thundering-herd problem after an outage and gives downstream systems time to drain their backlogs. Cap the backoff at a sane upper bound (often 5–15 minutes) and surface the retry count in logs.

Dead-letter queues

After N retries, route the message to a dead-letter queue rather than dropping it. The DLQ becomes a forensic record of what your system could not handle, and reviewing it weekly is one of the cheapest forms of operational hygiene.

Observability

Emit structured events at each step boundary: enqueue, start, success, failure, retry, dead-letter. Correlate by job id. If you only do one thing here, make sure the failure event carries the full error and the input that caused it.
